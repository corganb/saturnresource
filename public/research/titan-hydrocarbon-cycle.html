Titan's hydrocarbon cycle: surface lakes, atmospheric methane, and the Dragonfly mission

Review of Titan's methane-driven hydrocarbon cycle as resolved by Cassini-Huygens, including the polar lakes inventory, the atmospheric source-and-sink balance, and the Dragonfly mission objectives for Selk crater investigation.
2026-04-25 · 7 min read · Reviewed by Corgan Studio, Inc.

Introduction

Titan is the only Solar-System body other than Earth with stable liquid bodies on its surface. The lakes are filled with liquid methane and ethane rather than water, and they are part of an active hydrologic-equivalent cycle that includes evaporation, atmospheric transport, condensation, and precipitation. Cassini-Huygens (2004-2017) characterised the cycle from orbit and from the 2005 Huygens descent into Xanadu. This report reviews the cycle and the upcoming Dragonfly mission planned to resolve outstanding questions.

Polar lake inventory

Cassini's Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) instrument mapped Titan's polar regions during the 13-year mission, identifying more than 600 distinct lakes and seas at the north and south poles. The largest seas are Kraken Mare (approximately 400,000 square kilometres, larger than the Caspian Sea), Ligeia Mare, Punga Mare, and Ontario Lacus. Smaller lakes - typically 10-100 km across - are concentrated at the north pole, with fewer at the south pole; the asymmetry is interpreted as a present-day-orbital-eccentricity-driven preference for north-polar volatile residence.

The lakes are filled with methane-ethane mixtures. Cassini radiometry, plus radar attenuation studies, established lake depths up to 100 metres in Ligeia Mare and possibly 300 metres in Kraken Mare. Total liquid volume on Titan is estimated at approximately 70,000 cubic kilometres - several times the methane content of Earth's natural-gas reserves.

Atmospheric methane and the cycle

Titan's atmosphere is approximately 95 percent N2 and 5 percent CH4 at the surface, with trace heavier hydrocarbons. The atmospheric methane has a photochemical destruction lifetime of approximately 30 million years against UV-driven dissociation in the upper atmosphere. The current methane abundance therefore requires a sustained surface or interior source operating over the last 100 million years.

Two leading source mechanisms are debated. The cryovolcanic hypothesis posits that methane is supplied to the atmosphere through episodic outgassing from a subsurface ocean or hydrate-rich deep ice. The lake-evaporation hypothesis posits that surface lakes balance the evaporation-precipitation cycle, with methane sequestered in the deep ice between active periods. The two hypotheses are not mutually exclusive; both processes likely contribute.

Surface processes and the Huygens descent

The Huygens probe descended through Titan's atmosphere on January 14, 2005, transmitting data from a low-mid-latitude landing site in Xanadu region. Surface imagery showed water-ice "pebbles" with rounded morphology indicative of fluvial transport, and the surface compressional response of the probe penetrator implied a layered ice-sand composition consistent with periodic methane rainfall and runoff. The descent profile detected methane mole fraction increasing from approximately 1 percent in the upper atmosphere to 5 percent at the surface, demonstrating the methane source-sink balance directly.

Climate variability and seasonal change

Titan's 29.5-Earth-year orbital period and 26.7-degree obliquity produce strong seasonal climate variations. Cassini observations spanned approximately 40 percent of a Titan year and documented several seasonal phenomena: the migration of polar clouds between hemispheres, the methane rainfall pulse over Belet (south-mid-latitudes) in early Cassini years, and the depth changes in Ontario Lacus consistent with seasonal evaporation. Saturn-orbiter coverage will not resume until either a flagship Titan mission is implemented or a future orbital mission is proposed.

Dragonfly mission objectives

NASA's Dragonfly New Frontiers mission, planned for launch in 2028 and arrival in 2034, is a rotorcraft lander designed to operate at multiple Titan surface sites. The selected initial science target is the Selk crater region, an approximately 80-km-diameter impact crater in the equatorial dunes that is interpreted as a melt-pond / liquid-water environment shortly after impact. Dragonfly will fly between sites separated by 1-10 km, sampling surface composition by mass spectrometry, gamma-ray and neutron spectroscopy, and seismometer measurement.

The primary science questions Dragonfly addresses include: the chemical composition of Titan's surface organics (specifically whether prebiotic molecules of astrobiological interest are present), the depth and salinity of the subsurface water-ammonia ocean, and the present-day rate of methane outgassing from the interior. Dragonfly will not visit a polar lake but will provide the first ground-truth surface composition outside the Huygens descent location.

Outlook

Beyond Dragonfly, no Titan-orbit or follow-on lake-lander mission is currently in development. Telescopic monitoring with JWST and ground-based facilities continues to track atmospheric and large-scale surface evolution at decadal cadence. The next major Titan-data milestone is Dragonfly arrival in 2034.
